U2.W5: Cultural Blog: Pairing and Giving Feedback

Dev Bootcamp Blog

July 13th, 2015



Growing up with progressive education, group projects have always been a big part of our learning, so I'm somewhat used to working in a team and being able to communicate affectively. The part that I found difficult about DBC pairing is that everybody is at a different level of knowledge, so we are all at a different place, and it can be very intimidating sometimes. It can be rewarding as well becuase I am learning the technique, logic, and knowledge of another person, and it is always great to have other sources of knowledge. It can also be frustrating

Reading my feedback from my pairing sessions was very interesting. In many of my pairing sessions, I was very nervous, and thought that my nervousness would mask my knowledge of the material and curriculum. However, according to my feedback, it didnt -- which was rewarding to read. I already knew that I needed to work on my driving. For me, driving makes me very nervous becuase I want all my code to be correct and for it to make sense to my partner. I get passive becuase I am nervous that I am not giving the write code or that the other person is more knowledgable than I am, and would be able to tackle the problem with ease.

I believe that my feedback was helpful to my learning. Although I already knew the bit about driving, it gave me confidence to read that I am up to date with the materials, which gave me more condience to drive. Based on my feedback, I have tried to be better at driving. I always warn my partner that my feedback states that my driving skills need work, so that is something I am constantly trying to improve.

Writing feedback was also difficult. The biggest thing for me was writing the improvement section. I found that I have always learned something new from my pairing sesion, so it was hard for me to point out improvements when they helped improve me. I found though, that most of my comments were on pace and making sure that the partner understand. There were a few times in my pairing session where I felt my pair was moving on quickly after we finished the code without explanation. Although I asked them about it and did research, for the future they should be aware that everything needs to be explained to one another.

Overall, I really enjoy the idea of using pairing and giving feedback. I think using two totally different developed minds solving one problem is a great idea. Not only does it allow you to see the problem from a different view, but it also allows you to gain new techniques, knowledge and ideas about hwo to solve the problem. I also really enjoy the feedback aspect of pairing. I think getting feedback from a person who saw you in your groove, who saw you using your techniques and how you work with a pair is extrememly beneficial to any learning. I always try to grow from my feedback, and hope that my partners grow from the feedback I've given them.
